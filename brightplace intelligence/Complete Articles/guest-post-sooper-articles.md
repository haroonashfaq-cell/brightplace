# Temporary Housing in 2026: How Short-Term Leases and Sublets Actually Work

Not every renter needs a 12-month lease. Corporate relocations, travel nursing contracts, graduate school rotations, and remote work flexibility have created a growing population of renters who need housing for one to six months. The problem is that the rental market is still built for annual commitments. Finding a legitimate temporary housing option requires understanding two different arrangements: short-term leases and sublets.

Here is how each one works, what they cost, and what to watch for before you sign.

## Short-Term Leases: Fixed Dates, Clear Terms, Higher Cost

A short-term lease is a rental contract covering any stay shorter than 12 months, typically between 30 days and six months. It functions like a standard lease but with a compressed timeline and a higher monthly price.

The cost premium is the first thing renters notice. Short-term leases of three months or less typically cost 20 to 35 percent more per month than a comparable annual lease in the same building. A one-bedroom apartment that rents for $1,800 on a 12-month lease might run $2,250 to $2,430 on a three-month term. That premium pays for the flexibility of a shorter commitment and, in many cases, a furnished unit.

One legal distinction most renters miss: stays under 30 days are governed by hotel and transient occupancy law in most states, not by standard landlord-tenant law. That changes your rights. Security deposit return timelines, habitability standards, and formal eviction procedures may not apply if your stay is under that 30-day threshold.

Before signing any short-term rental agreement, read the cancellation policy and early termination clause carefully. These are the two sections most commonly misunderstood, and they apply from the moment your deposit is paid. A detailed breakdown of [what a short-term lease agreement actually covers](https://brightplace.ai/knowledgebase/short-term-lease-agreement) can help you understand each clause before committing.

## Sublets: Taking Over Someone Else's Lease

A sublet is a different arrangement entirely. Instead of signing a new lease with a landlord, you rent directly from an existing tenant who is temporarily leaving their apartment but wants to keep the lease active.

The existing tenant (the sublessor) remains on the original lease and retains the right to return when the sublet period ends. You (the sublessee) pay rent for the duration of your stay, usually at or near the original lease rate. This is the key financial advantage: sublets are often cheaper than short-term leases because the pricing reflects the original tenant's annual rate, not a short-term premium.

The legal requirements depend on where you are. In New York City, for example, tenants in buildings with four or more units have the legal right to request permission to sublet under state law. The landlord has 30 days to respond, and silence is treated as consent. The rules around [subletting in NYC](https://brightplace.ai/knowledgebase/sublet-apartments-nyc) are more structured than most renters expect, with specific requirements for written requests, landlord approval, and rent limits in stabilized apartments.

In other cities, sublet rules vary widely. Some landlords prohibit subletting entirely. Others allow it with written approval. Always check the original lease and get landlord consent in writing before paying any money.

## How to Choose Between the Two

The right option depends on three factors: your timeline certainty, your budget, and your tolerance for risk.

**Choose a short-term lease if:**

You know your exact dates and want a direct relationship with the landlord or property manager. Short-term leases have clearer legal protections (for stays over 30 days), professionally managed move-in and move-out processes, and no dependency on another tenant's reliability. The tradeoff is cost: you will pay a meaningful premium over annual rates.

**Choose a sublet if:**

You want lower monthly rent and are comfortable with a less formal arrangement. Sublets work well for renters who are flexible on exact dates and are willing to do more verification work upfront. The tradeoff is risk: you are dependent on the original tenant's lease being in good standing, and you have limited legal recourse if something goes wrong with the landlord relationship.

## What to Verify Before You Sign Either One

Regardless of which option you choose, verify these five things before paying any deposit:

1. **Read the cancellation policy.** Both short-term leases and sublets have cancellation terms. Know what happens if your plans change before you commit money.

2. **Confirm the lease allows what you are doing.** For sublets, this means written landlord approval. For short-term leases, this means confirming the property actually offers terms shorter than 12 months.

3. **Understand total cost.** Short-term leases may have admin fees, furniture rental charges, and utility costs not included in the listed rent. Sublets may require a separate security deposit to the original tenant.

4. **Get everything in writing.** Verbal agreements are not enforceable in most rental disputes. The lease, the sublet agreement, and the landlord approval should all be documented.

5. **Check which state's laws govern the agreement.** If you are crossing state lines for a temporary stay, the tenant protections available to you may be different from what you are used to at home.

## The Market Is Moving Toward Flexibility

The demand for temporary housing is not a trend. It reflects how work and life actually function in 2026. More employers offer relocation contracts measured in months, not years. More renters move between cities before committing to one. The rental market is slowly catching up, but the burden of understanding the options still falls on the renter.

Know the difference between a short-term lease and a sublet. Know what each one costs. Know what to verify before you pay. The flexibility is available if you know where to look.
