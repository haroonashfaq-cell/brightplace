# The brightplace Renter's Corner Content Guide

**Internal team playbook. Not for publication.**

How brightplace writes the conversational, expertise-driven content pieces that live in the Resources collection under the Renter's Corner category on brightplace.ai.

*Version 1.1, June 2026.*

---

## What changed in v1.1

Three additions, based on editorial review of the first three published drafts:

1. **Audience cohort context in every title.** Renter's Corner pieces now identify their audience cohort explicitly in the H1 title (e.g., "A Student's Guide" or "A Working Professional's Guide"). This is covered in the new section "Identifying and naming the audience cohort."

2. **Reader-centric narrative section headings.** The H2 that introduces the renter's story used to be "How [renter] came to brightplace." It now uses the format "How [renter], a [cohort + location], found [outcome]" so a reader in the same cohort recognizes themselves immediately. Covered in the section "The structure."

3. **Biographical grounding in the narrative setup.** The renter character is now introduced with more specific context (where they came from, what they are doing, where they are living) in the opening paragraph. Covered in the section "What to do with the source."

The rest of v1.0 stands as written. The PoE methodology, the brand rules, the Fair Housing constraints, the writing process, and the workflow have not changed.

---

## Why this format exists

The rental content market has been broken for years. Every major listing platform (we will not name them) publishes the same set of generic "how to" articles, rewritten quarterly by SEO contractors who have never actually helped a renter through a lease. AI has now made this content fully commoditized: ChatGPT, Perplexity, and Google AI Overviews surface the answer before anyone clicks a link, and competitors regenerate any commodity guide within days.

In that environment, writing more generic content is a losing strategy. The only thing that wins is content built on real, lived expertise that no outsider could fake.

brightplace has two assets that almost no other rental platform has:

1. **Real customer interactions.** Renters write to us. They leave reviews. They tell us specifically what went wrong with their search and what worked.
2. **Katie.** A named expert who has worked through hundreds of these conversations, who can speak in first person about what she actually sees in the market, and whose voice is consistent enough to become a recognizable brand asset.

The Renter's Corner format is what happens when you combine those two assets and write the result up for the open web. The goal is content that:

- Real renters find genuinely useful
- AI engines cite as the authoritative source on a topic
- Competitors cannot clone, because they do not have the source material or the named expert
- Builds Katie's authority over time, the way personal-finance writers like Suze Orman or Ramit Sethi built theirs

This is not a one-piece experiment. It is a content track that should produce 2 to 4 pieces a month indefinitely, each one seeded from a real customer story.

## What a Renter's Corner piece is

A Renter's Corner piece is a first-person article by Katie, structured around the real questions a real customer asked her, with each question becoming a section heading and Katie's answer underneath as a self-contained chunk. The piece identifies its audience cohort in the title, grounds the renter character with specific biographical context, and treats the reader as a member of the same cohort.

**Where it lives:** brightplace.ai/resources/[slug]
**Webflow collection:** Resources (not Guides)
**Category:** Renter's Corner
**Author byline:** Katie, Renter Education Lead at brightplace
**Length:** 1,500 to 2,200 words of published body content
**Reading time:** 7 to 10 minutes

**What it is not:**
- It is not a /guides/ piece. /guides/ has its own format (neighborhood guides, city guides, cohort guides) with stricter structural requirements (property callouts, comparison tables, "Operators to Explore" sections). Renter's Corner is looser and more editorial.
- It is not a dialogue. Earlier versions of this format were two-person conversations. We moved to single-author first-person because it extracts more cleanly for AI engines and reads better on the page.
- It is not a listicle. No "Top 5 Things to Know About X" framing. The structure is question-and-answer, not item-by-item ranking.
- It is not a generic explainer. Every piece is anchored in a specific real customer story.
- It is not audience-neutral. Every piece declares its audience in the title.

## The one test that decides everything

Before writing any sentence in a Renter's Corner piece, ask:

> "Could someone who has never done this work write this sentence?"

If the answer is yes, the sentence is commodity content. It will get copied by competitors and ignored by AI engines. Cut it or make it more specific.

If the answer is no (because the sentence contains a specific number, a real diagnostic, a named tool, a piece of reasoning that only an actual expert would know), keep it. Build the piece out of sentences like that.

Most of the published body should fail that test for an outsider. If you finish a draft and most of it could have been written by anyone, the piece is not ready.

This is the most important rule in the entire guide. Everything else exists to support it.

## Identifying and naming the audience cohort

Every Renter's Corner piece serves a specific reader cohort. The cohort is named in the H1 title, named in the reader-centric narrative section heading, and built into the biographical grounding of the renter character.

**How to identify the cohort:**

1. **Read the source review or conversation.** If the renter states their life stage or profession (Rachel said she was a student), use what they said.
2. **If the source is ambiguous, infer from context.** What is the typical search intent for this keyword? Who is the natural reader? A "bad credit + apartment" search trends toward working adults with past financial difficulty. A "pet-friendly + Panama City Beach" search trends toward working professionals or remote workers relocating.
3. **Flag every inferred cohort in the editorial notes.** When the cohort is not stated in the source, the inference should be reviewable. "Max's cohort (working professional in his late 20s) is an editorial inference, not stated in his original review."

**Cohorts we have used so far:**

- **Student** (Rachel, Bronx under $1,300) — explicit in source
- **Working Professional** (Max, bad credit; Cole, PCB pet-friendly) — inferred

**Other cohorts that will fit Renter's Corner pieces in the future:**

- Recent college graduate
- Remote worker or location-independent professional
- Military service member or family (Tyndall AFB, JBLM, Fort Liberty markets)
- Healthcare worker (travel nurses, residents, hospital staff)
- New parent or growing family
- Empty nester or downsizer
- First-time renter (anyone signing their first lease, regardless of profession)
- Recent transplant (someone who recently moved to a new city)
- Senior or retiree

**Rules for cohort selection:**

- The cohort must be one the renter source plausibly belongs to. Do not assign cohorts to fit a marketing goal rather than the actual story.
- The cohort cannot be a protected class under Fair Housing law (race, religion, national origin, sexual orientation, familial status, disability). Student and working professional are not protected classes and are permissible. "Single parent" or "Christian renter" would not be permissible.
- The cohort should be specific enough to be useful but general enough to capture meaningful search volume. "Working professional" works. "Mid-career marketing executive in their 30s" is too narrow.
- When in doubt, choose the broader cohort. A piece titled "A Working Professional's Guide" can still serve recent graduates and remote workers; a piece titled "A Mid-Career Marketing Manager's Guide" cannot serve much beyond its narrow segment.

## Title format with cohort context

Renter's Corner H1 titles follow this format:

> **[Topic with primary keyword]: A [Cohort]'s Guide**

Examples:

- "How to Get an Apartment with Bad Credit: A Working Professional's Guide"
- "How to Find Bronx Apartments for Rent Under $1,300: A Student's Guide"
- "How to Find Pet-Friendly Rentals in Panama City Beach: A Working Professional's Guide"

**Rules for title construction:**

- The primary keyword appears in the first half of the title before the colon
- The cohort designation appears after the colon in the format "A [Cohort]'s Guide"
- No clever framing, no rhetorical questions, no puns
- Total title length should stay under 70 characters where possible, since longer titles get truncated in search results
- If the cohort designation would push the title past 70 characters, drop a non-essential word from the keyword phrase (rarely needed)

**When a cohort would not naturally fit:**

If a piece serves a universal audience (for example, "How to Read an Apartment Lease" is universal across cohorts), use "A Renter's Guide" as the cohort fallback. Avoid leaving the title without a cohort tag entirely; even "A Renter's Guide" signals that the piece is for readers, not for landlords or operators.

## Source material is required (no exceptions)

A Renter's Corner piece does not start with a topic. It starts with a real source.

**Acceptable sources, in order of preference:**

1. **A real customer review.** Cole's review of brightplace.ai produced the Panama City Beach pet-friendly piece. Max's review produced the bad credit piece. Rachel's review produced the Bronx under $1,300 piece. These are the strongest seeds because they give you the renter's actual voice and actual outcome.

2. **A real customer support conversation.** A message Katie answered, anonymized as needed.

3. **A real recurring question pattern.** "Renters who message us asking about X almost always also ask about Y and Z." Katie can speak to patterns she has seen across many conversations even when she does not have a single named customer.

4. **A real anonymized case.** A specific renter's situation, with details changed enough to protect identity.

**Not acceptable:**

- "Imagine a renter who..."
- "Let's say someone is looking for..."
- "A hypothetical customer might ask..."
- Composite characters invented for the piece

If you find yourself building a piece without a real source, stop. The PoE methodology breaks without one. Ask Katie or the operations team for a real review or a real conversation that fits the topic.

## What to do with the source

Once you have a real customer review or conversation, you extract five things from it (updated from four in v1.0):

1. **The renter's voice.** One or two short phrases you will quote directly in the piece. These quoted phrases give the piece authenticity that no paraphrase can match.

2. **The renter's real situation.** What specifically were they dealing with? Job, savings, credit, budget, location, timing, what they had already tried?

3. **The renter's cohort.** Student, working professional, recent graduate, remote worker, etc. If the source states it, use what they said. If not, infer and flag.

4. **The renter's biographical context.** Where they came from, what they are doing, where they are living or moving. This becomes the opening sentence of the narrative setup paragraph, and the basis for the reader-centric section heading.

5. **The renter's outcome.** What happened? Did they find a place? How long did it take? Use whatever the source tells you. Do not invent operational details (which specific apartment, which exact workaround) that the source did not provide.

**Examples of biographical context done well:**

- "Rachel came to me as a student in New York City, looking for an affordable apartment in the Bronx that fit her budget and her commute to school." (Cohort: student. Location: NYC. Goal: budget + commute. All grounded in what the review actually states or strongly implies.)
- "Max came to me as a working professional in his late 20s, with a stable job paying $52,000 a year, savings of about $4,000, and good references from two previous landlords." (Cohort: working professional. Age: inferred. Financial context: stated in original draft.)
- "Cole came to me as a working professional moving to Panama City Beach with his pet." (Cohort: working professional. Geography: PCB. Constraint: has a pet. Movement direction: inferred.)

What the source does NOT tell you, you do not invent. If Max did not say which credit score range he was in, do not assign him one. If Cole did not say what kind of pet he had, do not give him a Labrador. Invented specifics break the PoE moat and create legal exposure (especially around real customers we are naming).

## The structure (in the exact order)

Every Renter's Corner piece follows this order. The order is doing work; do not rearrange it.

### 1. Editorial Note (review-only, not for publication)

A few sentences flagging what needs verification, what consent is pending, what Fair Housing concerns apply, and any unusual editorial judgment calls made in the draft. Always at the very top. Should now include a flag confirming whether the cohort was stated in the source or inferred.

### 2. SEO Metadata Block

Title tag, meta description, slug, canonical URL, schema specification, author byline, content track, category. Populated for Webflow CMS field mapping. The title tag matches the new H1 format with cohort context. The meta description should reference the cohort where natural.

### 3. H1 Title

Format: "[Topic with primary keyword]: A [Cohort]'s Guide." Primary keyword in the first half, cohort designation after the colon. See "Title format with cohort context" above.

### 4. Tagline (italic, one line)

A single descriptive sentence. Not a hook, not a tease. Tells the reader what they are about to get.

### 5. Metadata Card

Four-column table: Audience, Read Time, Updated, Topic. Webflow embed renders this as a teal-bordered card. The Audience column should match the cohort declared in the title.

### 6. The short answer (40 to 80 words)

A self-contained direct answer to the keyword query. This is what Google AI Overviews extract and cite. Specific numbers, date-stamped. Must work as a standalone paragraph if pulled out of context.

### 7. Reader-centric narrative section heading and setup (100 to 150 words)

The H2 introduces the renter's story in a way that signals to the cohort reader that the piece is for them.

**Heading format:** "How [renter], a [cohort + location], found [outcome]" or close variant.

Examples:
- "How Max, a working professional in his late 20s, rebuilt his rental options"
- "How Rachel, a student in New York City, found her Bronx apartment"
- "How Cole, a working professional moving to Panama City Beach, found a pet-friendly rental"

Under the heading, the narrative paragraph opens with biographical context (cohort, location, situation) and then transitions into the source's quoted phrases and the stakes that brought the renter to brightplace.

### 8. The customer questions as H2 sections (5 to 7 questions, 120 to 180 words each)

This is the substance of the piece. Each H2 is a real question phrased exactly as the renter would search it. Each answer starts with a direct-answer sentence, contains specific numbers throughout, shows diagnostic reasoning, acknowledges tradeoffs, and stands alone as a quotable chunk.

This is where Katie's expertise lives. Every section should have at least one specific number, one named tool or process, and one piece of reasoning that an outsider could not have produced.

### 9. What happened with [renter name] (60 to 100 words)

Brief narrative closing. What was the outcome? Use the source material's own words where possible. If the source does not specify, keep it general; do not invent. Acknowledge the outcome is one path, not a guarantee.

### 10. Key takeaways (60 to 100 words)

A compression of the whole piece into the few things that matter. Should make sense as a standalone paragraph if pulled out of context.

### 11. Frequently Asked Questions (4 to 6 Q&A pairs)

H3 questions, each phrased as a real search query. Answers 40 to 80 words, direct answer in sentence 1. Webflow embeds the FAQPage schema at the page template level.

### 12. Light conversion section (60 to 90 words)

A soft, useful invitation to use brightplace for the kind of search the piece addressed. Topic-appropriate H2 heading. No hard sell. No outcome guarantees.

### 13. Internal: Research Notes (not for publication)

VERIFY BEFORE PUBLISHING list, SOURCES USED list, FLAGS FOR FUTURE PIECES, EDITORIAL JUDGMENT NOTES. Now also includes the cohort inference flag if applicable.

### 14. Compliance Checklist Summary

A checkbox list confirming every rule below passed. Now includes the three new cohort-related checks.

## Katie's voice (first person, throughout)

Katie writes every Renter's Corner piece in first person. She is the brightplace Renter Education Lead. She has done this work for years. She has had thousands of conversations with renters.

**The voice patterns to maintain:**

- "When [renter] came to me," "In conversations I've had," "Most renters I work with," "I see this in roughly X of every Y renters"
- Specific numbers in nearly every paragraph
- Tradeoffs acknowledged honestly, not glossed over
- Named tools and resources
- Recent-experience references that suggest pattern recognition across many similar situations
- No condescension. The renter is treated as a competent adult dealing with an adult situation.

**Voice anti-patterns to catch and rewrite:**

- "It's worth noting": delete the phrase, the note still works
- "Many people wonder": start with the answer instead
- "In today's market": date-stamp specifically (Q2 2026) instead
- Passive voice ("the lease was reviewed"): use active ("I reviewed the lease")
- Rhetorical question openings ("Ever wondered what happens when..."): never. Real customer questions as H2 headings are the only acceptable question format.
- "Great question": never. Patronizing.

**The Katie sign-off requirement:**

Every Renter's Corner piece puts words in a real person's mouth. Katie must review and approve every first-person line attributed to her before the piece goes to staging. This is non-negotiable and is flagged in the Research Notes of every draft. The skill produces drafts, not finalized content. Katie owns the voice.

## The brand rules (non-negotiable)

A single violation of any rule below blocks publication. These are not preferences. They are gates.

### Em dashes

Zero tolerance. Never use — anywhere in body content. Also avoid `--` double hyphens. Use commas, periods, semicolons, colons, or parentheses depending on what the sentence actually needs.

### brightplace capitalization

Always lowercase. Even at the start of a sentence. Never "Brightplace," "BRIGHTPLACE," or "BRIGHTplace."

### Banned phrases

These never appear in body content:

| Banned | Use instead |
|---|---|
| signal (verb or noun) | indicator, suggests, points to, reflects |
| deep dive | walk-through, breakdown, closer look |
| navigate (metaphor) | work through, figure out, handle |
| landscape (metaphor) | market, environment, situation |
| unlock | open up, make possible |
| leverage (as verb) | use, draw on, take advantage of |
| whether you're X or Y | (rewrite the sentence entirely) |
| it's worth noting | (delete; the note still works) |
| arguably | (state the claim or do not) |
| hidden gem | (delete; describe the actual quality) |
| most underrated | (same; show, do not tell) |

### Banned sources

Never name or link to these in body content. They may appear in Research Notes for internal verification context.

- **ILS and marketplaces:** Apartments.com, Zillow, Trulia, Rent.com, Zumper, Apartment List, HotPads, RentCafe, Padmapper, Realtor.com, ForRent.com
- **Review platforms:** ApartmentRatings, Yelp, Google Reviews, Niche, AreaVibes, Crime Grade, Openigloo
- **Score aggregators:** Walk Score, Bike Score, Transit Score, GreatSchools
- **User forums:** Reddit, City-Data, BiggerPockets
- **Ranking sources:** NMHC rankings (any reference)

### Commercial vendors

Avoid naming specific commercial vendors in body content. Describe the category instead. Specific names may appear in Research Notes for verification only.

## Fair Housing (non-negotiable, applies to every piece)

These rules apply to every Renter's Corner piece regardless of topic.

- **No demographic framing.** Race, ethnicity, religion, national origin, sexual orientation, familial status, disability. Never describe a renter, place, or audience by who lives there.
- **No safety or crime language.** No "safe area," "low crime," "avoid after dark," or crime statistics.
- **No "gentrification" language.** Use market dynamics framing instead.
- **No religious density references.** No "lots of churches," "near the mosque."
- **No neighborhood identity by resident group.**
- **No K-12 school quality framing.** Universities can be mentioned as commute anchors. K-12 schools cannot be ranked or described qualitatively.
- **Income is math, not identity.**
- **No assumptions about why a renter has any particular financial situation.**

**Cohort targeting and Fair Housing:**

Naming an audience cohort in the title (student, working professional, remote worker) is permissible under Fair Housing because these are not protected classes. The piece can serve students; it can help students; it can be titled for students. What it cannot do is suggest that landlords should prefer students over other renters, that students deserve different rules, or that any neighborhood is "better for students" in a way that implies steering. The line: editorial content targeted at a cohort is fine. Housing recommendations that steer landlords toward or against a cohort are not.

**Pet-specific Fair Housing rule:** Pet ownership is NOT a federally protected class. Discussing breed restrictions and weight limits is fine. BUT: service animals and emotional support animals ARE protected under the Fair Housing Act and are explicitly not "pets." Any piece touching on pets must address this distinction clearly.

**When in doubt, ask.** Fair Housing law is complex, varies by jurisdiction, and matters more than any other compliance category.

## SEO and AEO requirements

Every piece must include:

- **Direct-answer opening sentence** in "The short answer" and at the start of every H2 question.
- **Audience cohort named in the H1 title.** Now a required field for AI engines to match user query intent.
- **Customer questions as H2 headings.** Phrase them exactly as the renter would search.
- **Self-contained quotable answer chunks** under each H2.
- **40 to 80 word paragraphs** for featured snippet eligibility.
- **Specific numbers, date-stamped.** "as of Q[N] 20XX." At least three specific numerical claims per H2 section.
- **FAQ section with question-format H3s.**
- **Active voice throughout.**
- **HowTo schema** when the topic is a sequential process.
- **Speakable schema** on the short answer, the H2 question blocks, and the FAQ.

## The writing process (in order)

1. **Verify real source material exists.** If not, stop and ask for one. Hard gate.
2. **Read the source carefully.** Identify the renter's real situation, real questions, real outcome, real quotable phrases, and (new in v1.1) cohort and biographical context.
3. **Identify or assign the audience cohort.** If the source states it, use what they said. If not, infer and flag.
4. **Write the H1 title** using the cohort-context format.
5. **Draft 5 to 7 customer questions** that would have come up in this real conversation.
6. **Write "The short answer" first** (40 to 80 word direct answer).
7. **Write the reader-centric narrative section heading** ("How [renter], a [cohort + location], found [outcome]").
8. **Write the narrative setup paragraph** opening with biographical context and including quoted phrases from the source.
9. **Write each H2 customer-question answer.**
10. **Write "What happened with [renter]"** using only what the source confirms or general framing.
11. **Write "Key takeaways"** as a stand-alone compression of the piece.
12. **Write the FAQ** (4 to 6 Q&A pairs).
13. **Write the light conversion section.**
14. **Write Research Notes** documenting verification needs, the cohort inference if any, and Katie sign-off.
15. **Run the compliance checks.** Fix anything that fails before delivering.

## The workflow: from review to published piece

1. **Source.** Real customer review or real customer question is identified, tagged with topic and cohort.
2. **Draft.** Writer (Claude Code via the renters-corner-writer skill, or a human writer following this guide) produces the markdown draft with cohort context, biographical grounding, and reader-centric heading.
3. **Self-check.** Compliance checks run on the draft.
4. **Editorial review.** A human reviewer reads the draft for voice, Fair Housing, factual plausibility, Katie-appropriateness, and cohort framing accuracy.
5. **Katie sign-off.** Katie reads every first-person line attributed to her and signs off or requests changes.
6. **Customer consent.** If a real customer is named, confirm consent.
7. **Completion work.** Claude Code runs the completion prompt for internal links, external links, image prompts, and data verification.
8. **Image sourcing.**
9. **Webflow draft.** The piece is created as a draft in the Resources collection with category Renter's Corner.
10. **Staging push.**
11. **Publish.**
12. **Post-publish housekeeping.** Sitemap.xml updated. llms.txt updated.

## Common mistakes (what review should catch)

These are the recurring failure modes when this format is executed without the full methodology applied:

1. **Composite renter character.** Always built from real source.
2. **Third-person framing of Katie.** Should be first-person.
3. **Flowing dialogue format.** Use the PoE question-as-heading shape.
4. **Generic conversion section.** Rewrite to match the topic and the voice.
5. **Vague numbers.** Always specific and date-stamped.
6. **Inventing source details.** Stay at the level of generality the source supports.
7. **Missing Katie sign-off flag.** Flag in Research Notes every time.
8. **Wrong URL path.** /resources/[slug], not /guides/[slug].
9. **Em dashes that slip through.** Single most common compliance failure.
10. **Fair Housing complacency.** When in doubt, ask.
11. **(New in v1.1) Missing cohort in the title.** Every piece needs "A [Cohort]'s Guide" in the H1.
12. **(New in v1.1) Generic narrative section heading.** "How [renter] came to brightplace" alone is no longer enough; the heading should include cohort and location.
13. **(New in v1.1) Thin biographical context.** The narrative setup paragraph should ground the renter in their cohort, location, and situation in the first sentence.

## What needs to happen to scale this

We currently have three published Renter's Corner pieces (now updated to v1.1 format) and a skill that produces drafts cleanly. To make this a real content track that produces 2 to 4 pieces a month indefinitely, we need:

1. **A real customer review pipeline.** The operations team should set up consent-based collection of renter stories at signing or at lease success, tagged by topic and cohort.

2. **A cohort taxonomy.** The list under "Identifying and naming the audience cohort" is a starting point. The team should maintain a working document of approved cohorts and decline new ones that fragment the audience too narrowly.

3. **A Katie review cadence.** Establish a weekly batch review of drafts and a written voice guide for her.

4. **An image sourcing process.** Pre-built library of brand-appropriate stock images organized by topic and cohort.

5. **A verification source list.** Single internal source-of-truth document that every Renter's Corner piece references, updated quarterly.

6. **A topic and keyword pipeline.** Match incoming customer reviews to keyword opportunities so each new review fuels a topic that already has search demand validated.

7. **A retire-and-update process.** Every piece needs review every 6 months.

## Closing note

Renter's Corner is not a marketing format. It is an attempt to be the most useful, most honest source on the internet for renter education topics, built on the assumption that the most useful thing you can do for a stranger searching for help is to actually help them. The format works because it is built on real experience, written by a real expert, declared for a real audience, and respects the reader as a competent adult.

If we keep producing pieces that pass the governing test (most sentences would fail the "could an outsider write this" test) and that name their audience clearly, we will build something that compounds over time. Each piece increases brightplace's authority. Each piece becomes a citation source for AI engines. Each piece helps a real renter solve a real problem. None of those things happen if we cut corners on the source material, the voice, the cohort identification, or the compliance rules.

When in doubt, ask the question this entire methodology rests on:

> "Could someone who has never done this work write this sentence?"

If yes, fix it.
